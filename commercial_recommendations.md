# Olist Marketplace: Commercial Recommendations
**Prepared for:** Merchandising and Operations leadership
**Author:** Joanne Thai
**Date:** June 5th 2026

## Executive Summary
Late deliveries devastate review scores on Olist, with approximately 75% of orders delayed 4+ days receiving 1 or 2-star reviews. However, lateness surprisingly does not reduce repeat purchase, while early boosts it by 31%. The operational lever therefore is not 'avoid being late', it is 'improve the delivery process so more orders arrive early'. For a marketplace with a structural 3% repeat rate, this is one of the few retention levers in the business. 
Underneath that finding, Olist's economics are acquisition-driven, not retention-driven when 94% of revenue comes from customers who order only once, with retention sitting at a structural ~3% baseline rather than declining. Within those constraints, the biggest commercial lever sits in operational quality within growing categories. The 'Fix Experience' quadrant alone that accounts for 35% of marketplace revenue was primarily damaged by carrier-transit failures rather than seller delays. The recommendations below address both challenges, which are targeted operational improvements in the four highest-leverage categories (bed_bath_table, watches_gifts, furniture_decor, office_furniture), and a re-framing of Olist's delivery promise to convert more orders into 'early' status. 

## Marketplace Overview
Olist is a Brazilian e-commerce marketplace that sells via small independent sellers, not direct from brands or first-party inventory. This dataset covers ~99K orders from ~93K customers over the period from September 2016 to August 2018, generating 15.4M BRL in marketplace revenue across 73 product categories and approximately 3,000 independent sellers. As a fragmented marketplace where transactions occur with many small sellers rather than a single retailer, customer-to-marketplace loyalty is structurally low, which is a defining characteristic of the business model.

## Key Findings
- **Olist's most valuable customer segment is also its most inactive.** The "Cannot Lose Them" segment, accounts for 15.5% of customers who historically drove 29% of the revenue, has now gone dormant, with the average recency of 393 days. This is the largest single retention asset in the business that no one is currently engaging. 

- **Housewares is the marketplace's standout growth opportunity.** With 90% growth, a 4.11 review score, and 452 distinct sellers, it sits squarely in the "Invest More" quadrant, which is large enough to matter (758K BRL), growing fast, and operationally healthy. Most "Invest More" categories are smaller, so housewares is the rare combination of scale and momentum.

- **Late deliveries are a carrier problem, not a seller problem.** The difference between on-time and late_4plus orders is almost entirely in the carrier transit phase (8 → 32 days). The customer experience cost is severe, with a 2.17-star average drop vs on-time deliveries (4.03 → 1.86).

- **Customer experience deficits cluster geographically** Nine Brazilian states average below the 4.08 national review benchmark, with 8 are in the north/northeast. The surprise is Rio de Janeiro being the second-largest state market (12,211 orders, 2.03M BRL) but rated below average, suggesting an urban-density problem distinct from rural-distance challenge in the north. 

## Prioritised Recommendations
### 1. Prioritise operational improvement in four high-revenue, low-rated categories

**Finding:** The "Fix Experience" quadrant has 15 categories that generate 5.31M BRL, accounts for 35% of the marketplace total revenue. Among those, the top 4 highest-revenue are bed_bath_table (1.22M), watches_gifts (1.26M), furniture_decor(880K), and office_furniture (335K). All of these 4 categories have below-average review scores, but office_furniture with only 3.52 score is the standout problem. 

**Action:** Conduct a 60-day diagnostic across these four categories. The diagnostic should examine the freight quality, packaging, product-description accuracy, and top-complaint sellers. Office furniture should be the first priority given the severity of the issue. 

**Estimated Impact:** These 4 categories represent 3.7M BRL in revenue across the dataset window (~24% of total market revenue) and currently average a review score of 3.87, which is 0.2 score below the marketplace benchmark (4.07). A conservative target of closing half that gap would deliver measurable improvement to marketplace satisfaction while addressing systemic quality issues in categories where customer demand is robust despite experience problems.

**Complexity:** Medium. This requires coordination across merchandising, operations, and seller-relations teams, but no new systems or contracts. This is fully reversible as the diagnostic itself commits Olist to nothing.

### 2. Carrier portfolio overhaul focused on the 9 underperforming states
 
**Finding:** Late deliveries are fundamentally a carrier problem. The carrier-transit phase consumes 74% of total customer wait time on average and quadruples between early and severely-late orders (from 7.9 days to 31.8 days), while seller handover only doubles. Customer experience deficits also cluster geographically, with 8 of the 9 worst-rated states located in the north and northeast. Rio de Janeiro is the exception. It is the second largest market place by volume (12,211 orders, 2.03M BRL) but rated below the marketplace average at 3.97. 

**Action:** Conduct a regional carrier audit across the 9 underperforming states within 90 days. Identify the carriers responsible for the worst-tail transit times in each region and assess whether the issue is a single underperforming partner, a structural distance challenge, or last-mile complexity. Where viable, replace or supplement existing carrier relationships with regional alternatives. Rio de Janeiro warrants its own diagnostic given the urban density issue, which is likely distinct from the rural-distance problem in the north and northeast.

**Estimated Impact:** Addresses operational quality in regions representing approximately 23% of marketplace revenue, with Rio de Janeiro alone accounting for 13.2%. Improvements to carrier transit time would compound across two metrics already shown to be tightly linked, namely review scores (which drop 2.17 stars from on-time to severly-late deliveries) and repeat purchase rate (which lifts 31% when first delivery arrives early rather than on time).

**Complexity:** High. This requires coordination across operations, legal, and regional teams, and carrier contract changes are partially irreversible commitments. A staged approach focused on the 2-3 highest-revenue underperforming states first would reduce exposure while validating the operational thesis before broader rollout. 

### 3. Tighten the delivery promise to convert "on-time" into "early"

**Finding:** Olist's delivery time estimates are conservatively long, meaning "on-time" deliveries average 19 days. By contrast, the 87,528 early deliveries average 10.9 days, which is much closer to actual customer expectations. The wide gap between these two classifications, combined with the finding that early deliveries boost repeat purchase rates by 31% relative to on-time, suggests that the customer-facing promise is generous to the point of weakening its own commercial value. 

**Action:** Tighten the delivery time estimation algorithm as there's room to make "on-time" a more meaningful commitment without sacrificing operational reliability. Test in a region or category first, then monitor the share of orders falling into each delivery bucket as the new estimate take effect. Expand gradually once the impact on customer satisfaction and repeat behaviour is validated.

**Estimated Impact:** Shifting some share of on-time orders into "early" classification would create modest but real lift to the structural low repeat rate. Given the 31% relative lift between the two buckets, even a partial reclassification of on-time orders would meaningfully affect overall marketplace repeat behaviour.

**Complexity:** Low. This is a zero-cost lever with only UX and algorithm adjustments required, and no carrier or seller coordination. It is fully reversible because no contracts or partnerships are involved. 

### 4. Win-back campaign for the "Cannot Lose Them" segment

**Finding:** The "Cannot Lose Them" segment, with 14,428 customers, historically drove 29% of the marketplace revenue (~4.47M BRL), giving it a revenue index of 1.9x. This segment is a high-value pool, not just dormant customers. The 1.9x index means each Cannot Lose Them customer was historically worth nearly twice the average, and it justifies a higher acquisition-style spend than a typical dormant outreach campaign. 

**Action:** Launch a targeted reactivation email/discount campaign with high-value offer as their historic CLV justifies a meaningful incentive. Given the segment's average historic order value of approximately 310 BRL, offers in the range of 30 to 50 BRL discounts (10-15% of historic value) are within the economically defensible range. Test with a small subset of the segment first, measure response rates against a control group, and refine before broader rollout. 

**Estimated Impact:** Conservative reactivation rates on high-value customers still produce meaningful revenue. If even 2% of the segment reactivates with at least one purchase at historic average value, that translates to approximately 290 reactivated customers and 89K BRL in recovered revenue, representing efficient use of marketing spend compared to acquiring new customers to match that historic value. 

**Complexity:** Low to medium. The campaign itself requires no new infrastructure, but designing the test, defining the offer structure, and analysing the response will require coordination between marketing and analytics teams over a 4 to 6 weeks cycle. 

## What's Next
Several questions emerged during this analysis that would meaningfully sharpen the recommendations given additional data or time. 

- **Rio de Janeiro's underperformance**. As the second-largest market by volume but rated below average, Rio de Janeiro is a meaningful commercial exposure. Understanding whether the issue is urban density, specific carriers failures, last-mile complexity, or something else would inform whether RJ needs a different operational solution than the rural north and northeast. 

- **Survivor bias in retention finding**. The finding that late_4plus orders return at a slightly higher rate than on-time (2.55% vs 2.33%) is counterintuitive. One hypothesis is that customer service recovery efforts (refunds, free shipping codes, complaint resolution) actively won back the worst-experience customers. A follow-up combining order data with customer service interaction data would clarify the picture. 

- **Marketing spend/acquisition cost data**. Several recommendations assume that retention is structurally low and acquisition is the dominant growth lever. Verifying this with real customer acquisition cost, including channel-level breakdowns, would let us compute per-customer ROI and refine the recommendation to favour acquisition-led growth versus dormant-customer reactivation. 

- **Seller-level performance**. The category-level recommendation assumes operational issues are spread across all sellers in these categories. Seller-level review analysis might reveal that a small number of problematic sellers account for most negative reviews, which would significantly simplify the operational fix. 

## Methodology Note
This analysis uses Olist's public dataset covering September 2016 to August 2018 (~24 months). All revenue is reported in Brazilian Reais (BRL), no USD conversion has been applied. The dataset is filtered to `order_status = 'delivered'` for all customer-facing metrics. For customer-level analysis, `customer_unique_id` (per-person) was used instead of `customer_id` (per-order). The growth windows were computed based on prior 6 months from Sep 2017-Feb 2018 and recent 6 months from Mar 2018-Aug 2018.

Two product categories retained their original Portuguese names (`portateis_cozinha_e_preparadores_de_alimentos` and `fashio_female_clothing`) due to missing entries in the category translation table. Combined, these account for less than 0.1% of marketplace revenue. They appear in their original Portuguese names within the category analysis.

The data set-up and EDA phase utilise dual implementation of pandas-only and MySQL approach. All codebase available at https://github.com/joanne-thai/olist_commercial_analytics
